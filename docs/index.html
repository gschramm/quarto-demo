MLEM Convergence Proof with Full Rank Assumption

A self-contained proof for \(y \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b})\)

Introduction

The MLEM (Maximum Likelihood Expectation Maximisation) algorithm is the standard reconstruction method in emission tomography (PET, SPECT). It produces non-negative images and steadily improves the fit to the measured data at every iteration. But does it always find the non-negative maximum likelihood image? And if so, why?

This document gives a complete, self-contained proof that MLEM converges to the unique non-negative image that maximises the Poisson log-likelihood. We assume only basic calculus and probability. Every mathematical step is explained.

Setup

The forward model

We measure \(I\) detector counts \(y_1, \ldots, y_I \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) (the sinogram). The image to be recovered has \(J\) voxels with (unknown) activities \(x_1, \ldots, x_J \geq 0\), collected into a column vector \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^J\).

The forward model relates image to expected counts:

\[\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^{J} a_{ij}\, x_j + b_i = (A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b})_i, \qquad i = 1,\ldots,I,\]

where:

  • \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{I \times J}_{\geq 0}\) is the system matrix. Entry \(a_{ij}\) is the probability that an event emitted in voxel \(j\) is detected in bin \(i\).
  • \(\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^I_{\geq 0}\) is the background (scatter and random coincidences), assumed known and fixed.

We model each detector bin as an independent Poisson random variable:

\[y_i \sim \operatorname{Poisson}\!\bigl(\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x})\bigr), \quad \text{independently for all } i.\]

The log-likelihood

The probability of observing count \(y_i\) when the expected count is \(\bar{y}_i\) is \(P(y_i) = e^{-\bar{y}_i}\,\bar{y}_i^{y_i}/y_i!\). Taking the logarithm and summing over all bins (and dropping terms that do not depend on \(\mathbf{x}\)) gives the Poisson log-likelihood:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{I} \Bigl[y_i \log \bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x})\Bigr].\]

Our goal is to find the image \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) that maximises \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\) subject to \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\) (non-negativity).

The MLEM update

Starting from an initial image \(\mathbf{x}^0 > \mathbf{0}\), MLEM produces a sequence of images \(\mathbf{x}^0, \mathbf{x}^1, \mathbf{x}^2, \ldots\) via the update rule:

\[\boxed{x_j^{k+1} = \frac{x_j^k}{\displaystyle\sum_i a_{ij}} \sum_{i=1}^{I} a_{ij}\, \frac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}, \qquad j = 1,\ldots,J.}\]

The ratio \(y_i / \bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)\) compares measured to predicted counts in bin \(i\): it equals 1 when the current estimate is perfect, is greater than 1 when the model under-predicts, and is less than 1 when it over-predicts. These correction factors are back-projected into image space and normalised by the scanner sensitivity \(\sum_i a_{ij}\).

The full-rank assumption

We assume throughout that \(A\) has full column rank, meaning no non-zero vector \(\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}\) satisfies \(A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\). Practically this means:

  1. Every voxel contributes to at least one detector bin (\(\sum_i a_{ij} > 0\) for all \(j\)).
  2. No two voxels have identical detection patterns.

Both conditions are satisfied for any realistic scanner geometry.

Proof Roadmap

The proof consists of five steps, each building on the previous.

NoteThe five things we need to show

  1. Bounded above: The log-likelihood \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\) has a finite maximum over \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\).

  2. Non-negativity preserved: If \(\mathbf{x}^k \geq \mathbf{0}\), then \(\mathbf{x}^{k+1} \geq \mathbf{0}\).

  3. Monotonic increase: Each update satisfies \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\).

  4. Bounded iterates: The sequence \(\{\mathbf{x}^k\}\) stays within a bounded region (i.e. the images do not grow without bound).

  5. Convergence to the constrained ML solution: The sequence \(\mathbf{x}^k\) converges to the unique maximiser \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) of \(\mathcal{L}\) over \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\).

Steps 1–3 together imply that \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\) is a monotonically increasing sequence with an upper bound, so it converges to some value \(\mathcal{L}^*\). Steps 4 and 5 then show that the iterates themselves converge, and that their limit is the correct answer.

Step 1: The Log-Likelihood is Bounded Above

The Poisson log-likelihood can be rewritten as:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i \Bigl[y_i \log \bar{y}_i - \bar{y}_i\Bigr] = \sum_i \bigl(y_i \log y_i - y_i\bigr) - \underbrace{\sum_i \Bigl[y_i \log\tfrac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i} + \bar{y}_i - y_i\Bigr]}_{\geq\; 0}.\]

To see this, write \(y_i \log \bar{y}_i = y_i \log y_i - y_i\log(y_i/\bar{y}_i)\) and rearrange. The underlined term is a sum of generalised Kullback–Leibler divergences \(d(y_i, \bar{y}_i) = y_i \log(y_i/\bar{y}_i) + \bar{y}_i - y_i\), each of which is non-negative (this follows from \(\log u \leq u - 1\) for all \(u > 0\), applied with \(u = \bar{y}_i/y_i\)). Therefore:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \leq \sum_i \bigl(y_i \log y_i - y_i\bigr) =: \mathcal{L}_{\max} < \infty.\]

The upper bound \(\mathcal{L}_{\max}\) is finite and is achieved only when \(\bar{y}_i = y_i\) for all \(i\) — i.e. when the predicted counts exactly match the measurements. \(\square\)

TipConvention: \(0 \log 0 = 0\)

Whenever a detector bin has zero measured counts (\(y_i = 0\)), the term \(y_i \log y_i\) in \(\mathcal{L}_{\max}\) is formally \(0 \cdot (-\infty)\). We adopt the standard convention

\[0 \log 0 := 0,\]

justified by the fact that \(\lim_{y \to 0^+} y \log y = 0\) (the function is continuous from the right at zero).

Step 2: MLEM Preserves Non-Negativity

Inspect the update formula: if \(x_j^k \geq 0\) and all \(a_{ij} \geq 0\) and \(y_i \geq 0\), then every factor in the product is non-negative, so \(x_j^{k+1} \geq 0\). Since we start with \(\mathbf{x}^0 \geq \mathbf{0}\), all iterates satisfy \(\mathbf{x}^k \geq \mathbf{0}\) by induction. \(\square\)

Step 3: Each MLEM Update Increases the Log-Likelihood

This is the most important step, and it uses the EM (Expectation–Maximisation) framework.

The complete-data idea

Maximising \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\) directly is hard because the log contains a sum over all voxels, \(\log \bar{y}_i = \log(\sum_j a_{ij} x_j + b_i)\), which couples all unknowns together.

The EM trick is to imagine that we could observe more data than we actually do. In PET/SPECT, every detected event originated from some voxel \(j\), but the detector only tells us the bin \(i\) — not the voxel. If we also knew the voxel of origin, the problem would factor into independent sub-problems, one per voxel.

Define the complete data \(n_{ij}\) as the (hypothetical, unobserved) number of events detected in bin \(i\) that originated from voxel \(j\), plus \(n_{i0}\) for background events in bin \(i\). These satisfy:

\[n_{ij} \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(a_{ij}\, x_j), \quad j \geq 1, \qquad n_{i0} \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(b_i), \qquad \text{all independent,}\]

and they sum to the observed data: \(y_i = \sum_{j=0}^{J} n_{ij}\).

The complete-data log-likelihood is much simpler than \(\mathcal{L}\):

\[\log p(\mathbf{n} \mid \mathbf{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \left[\log x_j \underbrace{\sum_i n_{ij}}_{\text{counts from voxel }j} - x_j \underbrace{\sum_i a_{ij}}_{s_j}\right] + \text{const},\]

which is a sum over voxels — each \(x_j\) appears independently.

The \(Q\) function

Since \(\mathbf{n}\) is not observed, we cannot use \(\log p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{x})\) directly. Instead, EM uses its conditional expectation given the observed data \(\mathbf{y}\) and the current estimate \(\mathbf{x}^k\):

\[Q(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{x}^k) = E\!\left[\log p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{x}) \;\Big|\; \mathbf{y},\, \mathbf{x}^k\right].\]

Reading the notation: \(E[\,\cdot\mid \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x}^k]\) means “the average value of \((\cdot)\), computed over all possible complete-data configurations \(\mathbf{n}\) that are consistent with the observed counts \(\mathbf{y}\), weighted by how probable each configuration is under the current image estimate \(\mathbf{x}^k\).” In short: we average out the unknown \(\mathbf{n}\) using our best current guess \(\mathbf{x}^k\).

To evaluate \(Q\) we need the conditional expectation of \(n_{ij}\). One can show using the Poisson splitting property - see Section 10.1 - that:

\[E[n_{ij} \mid y_i, \mathbf{x}^k] = \frac{a_{ij}\, x_j^k}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}\, y_i =: \hat{n}_{ij}^k.\]

Intuitively: the \(y_i\) observed events in bin \(i\) are distributed among voxels proportionally to each voxel’s contribution to the expected counts \(\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)\). Substituting:

\[Q(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{x}^k) = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \left[\hat{n}_j^k \log x_j - s_j x_j\right] + \text{const}, \qquad \hat{n}_j^k = \sum_i \hat{n}_{ij}^k.\]

This is again separable in the \(x_j\), so the maximisation over \(\mathbf{x}\) can be done voxel by voxel.

The fundamental identity

Using Bayes’ theorem, we can write the joint distribution of \(\mathbf{n}\) in two ways:

\[p(\mathbf{n} \mid \mathbf{x}) = p(\mathbf{y} \mid \mathbf{x}) \cdot p(\mathbf{n} \mid \mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x}).\]

Taking logarithms:

\[\log p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{x}) = \underbrace{\log p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{x})}_{\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})} + \log p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}).\]

Now take the conditional expectation \(E[\,\cdot\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}^k]\) of both sides. Since \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) = \log p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{x})\) does not involve \(\mathbf{n}\), its expectation is just itself. Defining \(H(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) = E[\log p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x})\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}^k]\):

\[Q(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) = \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) + H(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k).\]

Rearranging gives the fundamental identity:

\[\boxed{\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) = Q(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) - H(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k).}\]

The EM guarantee

Each EM iteration sets \(\mathbf{x}^{k+1} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{x} \geq 0}\, Q(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\) (shown below to give exactly the MLEM formula). We want to show \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\).

Using the fundamental identity at both \(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}\) and \(\mathbf{x}^k\):

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}) - \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) = \underbrace{\bigl[Q(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) - Q(\mathbf{x}^k\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\bigr]}_{\text{Term A}} + \underbrace{\bigl[H(\mathbf{x}^k\mid\mathbf{x}^k) - H(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\bigr]}_{\text{Term B}}.\]

Term A \(\geq 0\): By definition, \(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}\) maximises \(Q(\cdot\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\), so \(Q(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) \geq Q(\mathbf{x}^k\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\).

Term B \(\geq 0\): Expanding the difference:

\[H(\mathbf{x}^k\mid\mathbf{x}^k) - H(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) = E\!\left[\log \frac{p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}^k)}{p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x})}\;\Bigg|\;\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}^k\right] = \underbrace{D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}^k)\;\|\;p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x})\bigr)}_{\geq\; 0}.\]

The Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) = \sum_{\mathbf{n}} p(\mathbf{n}) \log \frac{p(\mathbf{n})}{q(\mathbf{n})}\) is always \(\geq 0\), with equality only when \(p = q\).

Therefore \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}) - \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) \geq 0\), i.e. the log-likelihood never decreases. \(\square\)

NoteWhy the KL divergence is non-negative

For any two probability distributions \(p\) and \(q\), the KL divergence satisfies:

\[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) = \sum_{\mathbf{n}} p(\mathbf{n})\log\frac{p(\mathbf{n})}{q(\mathbf{n})} \geq 0.\]

This follows from Jensen’s inequality applied to the concave function \(\log\):

\[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) = -\sum_{\mathbf{n}} p(\mathbf{n})\log\frac{q(\mathbf{n})}{p(\mathbf{n})} \geq -\log\!\left(\sum_{\mathbf{n}} p(\mathbf{n})\cdot\frac{q(\mathbf{n})}{p(\mathbf{n})}\right) = -\log 1 = 0.\]

The M-step gives the MLEM update

Maximise \(Q(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) = \sum_j [\hat{n}_j^k \log x_j - s_j x_j] + \text{const}\) over \(x_j \geq 0\). Setting the derivative to zero:

\[\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x_j} = \frac{\hat{n}_j^k}{x_j} - s_j = 0 \quad\Longrightarrow\quad x_j^{k+1} = \frac{\hat{n}_j^k}{s_j} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_i \hat{n}_{ij}^k}{\displaystyle\sum_i a_{ij}} = \frac{x_j^k}{\displaystyle\sum_i a_{ij}} \sum_i a_{ij}\,\frac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}.\]

This is exactly the MLEM formula, confirming that MLEM is an EM algorithm. \(\square\)

Step 4: The Iterates Are Bounded

Steps 1–3 tell us that \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\) is a monotonically increasing sequence with \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) \leq \mathcal{L}_{\max}\). In particular, all iterates lie in the sub-level set:

\[\mathcal{S} = \{\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} : \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^0)\}.\]

We now show that \(\mathcal{S}\) is bounded (and therefore compact).

Why \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \to -\infty\) as \(\|\mathbf{x}\| \to \infty\): The log-likelihood contains the term \(-\sum_i \bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}) = -\mathbf{1}^T A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{1}^T\mathbf{b}\). When \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\) and \(\|\mathbf{x}\| \to \infty\), at least one component \(x_j \to \infty\). Because \(A\) has full column rank, the column \(a_{\cdot j}\) is not zero, so \(\sum_i a_{ij} x_j \to \infty\). Therefore \(\sum_i \bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}) \to \infty\), which drives \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \to -\infty\).

Since \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \to -\infty\) for large \(\|\mathbf{x}\|\) and \(\mathcal{S}\) requires \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^0) > -\infty\), the set \(\mathcal{S}\) must be bounded.

Conclusion: All iterates \(\mathbf{x}^k\) lie in the bounded set \(\mathcal{S}\). In particular, no voxel value can grow without bound. \(\square\)

Step 5: Convergence to the Constrained ML Solution

We now combine all previous steps to show that \(\mathbf{x}^k\) converges to the unique constrained ML solution \(\hat{\mathbf{x}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\).

The log-likelihood values converge

From Steps 1 and 3: \(\{\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\}\) is monotonically increasing and bounded above. By the monotone convergence theorem (a real-analysis result for sequences of real numbers), it converges:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) \;\longrightarrow\; \mathcal{L}^* \;\leq\; \mathcal{L}_{\max}.\]

A convergent subsequence exists

From Step 4, all iterates lie in the bounded set \(\mathcal{S} \subset \mathbb{R}^J\). By the Bolzano–Weierstrass theorem (every bounded sequence in \(\mathbb{R}^J\) has a convergent subsequence), there exists a subsequence \(\mathbf{x}^{k_1}, \mathbf{x}^{k_2}, \ldots\) converging to some limit point \(\mathbf{x}^*\):

\[\mathbf{x}^{k_m} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbf{x}^* \quad \text{as } m \to \infty.\]

Since \(\mathcal{L}\) is continuous and \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) \to \mathcal{L}^*\):

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \lim_{m\to\infty} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k_m}) = \mathcal{L}^*.\]

The limit point is the constrained ML solution

\(\mathbf{x}^*\) achieves \(\mathcal{L}^*\): Since \(\mathcal{L}\) is continuous and \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k_m}) \to \mathcal{L}^*\), we have \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathcal{L}^*\).

\(\mathbf{x}^*\) is the global maximum: Suppose for contradiction that \(\mathbf{x}^*\) is not the global maximum \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\). Because \(\mathcal{L}\) is strictly concave under full rank (its Hessian \(\nabla^2\mathcal{L} = -A^\top D A\), with \(D = \operatorname{diag}(y_i/\bar{y}_i^2) > 0\), is negative definite), it has a unique global maximum. Since \(\mathbf{x}^* \neq \hat{\mathbf{x}}\), applying one MLEM step from \(\mathbf{x}^*\) must strictly increase \(\mathcal{L}\) (the EM guarantee holds with strict inequality whenever the current point is not the maximiser of \(Q\), which occurs here because \(Q\) is also strictly concave):

\[\mathcal{L}(F(\mathbf{x}^*)) > \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathcal{L}^*.\]

But \(F\) is continuous, so \(\mathcal{L}(F(\mathbf{x}^{k_m})) \to \mathcal{L}(F(\mathbf{x}^*)) > \mathcal{L}^*\), contradicting \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) \to \mathcal{L}^*\). Therefore \(\mathbf{x}^* = \hat{\mathbf{x}}\).

The full sequence converges

Every convergent subsequence of \(\{\mathbf{x}^k\}\) has the same limit \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) (by the argument above). Since the full sequence is bounded (Step 4), the Bolzano–Weierstrass theorem guarantees it has at least one convergent subsequence, and since all such subsequences share the unique limit \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\), the full sequence converges:

\[\mathbf{x}^k \;\longrightarrow\; \hat{\mathbf{x}} \quad \text{as } k \to \infty. \quad \square\]

Summary

NoteConvergence theorem

Let \(y_i \sim \operatorname{Poisson}((A\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{b})_i)\) with \(A\) having full column rank and \(b_i \geq 0\). Starting from any \(\mathbf{x}^0 > \mathbf{0}\), the MLEM iterates \(\mathbf{x}^k\) converge to the unique global maximiser \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) of the Poisson log-likelihood over \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\).

The proof proceeded in five steps:

Step What we showed Key tool
1 \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\) is bounded above \(\log u \leq u - 1\)
2 \(\mathbf{x}^k \geq \mathbf{0}\) for all \(k\) MLEM formula
3 \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\) EM framework (Q function + KL \(\geq 0\))
4 \(\{\mathbf{x}^k\}\) stays bounded Full rank \(\Rightarrow\) compact level sets
5 \(\mathbf{x}^k \to \hat{\mathbf{x}}\) (constrained ML solution) Bolzano–Weierstrass + strict concavity (full rank)

The most important step is Step 3: the EM framework shows that MLEM always makes progress, and the KL divergence is the reason why. Steps 4 and 5 guarantee that this progress is not wasted — the algorithm does not wander off to infinity and the improvements accumulate to reach the true answer.

Appendix

The conditional expectation via Poisson splitting

Setup. In the complete-data model each voxel \(j\) contributes independently to detector bin \(i\): \(n_{ij} \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(a_{ij}x_j)\), and the observed count is the total \(y_i = \sum_j n_{ij}\).

Poisson splitting property. Let \(N_1, \ldots, N_J\) be independent Poisson random variables with means \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_J\), and let \(N = N_1 + \cdots + N_J \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda)\), \(\lambda = \sum_j \lambda_j\). Then the conditional distribution of \((N_1, \ldots, N_J)\) given \(N = n\) is multinomial:

\[(N_1, \ldots, N_J) \mid N = n \;\sim\; \operatorname{Multinomial}\!\left(n;\; \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda}, \ldots, \frac{\lambda_J}{\lambda}\right).\]

Proof. The joint probability of \((N_1 = n_1, \ldots, N_J = n_J)\) with \(\sum_j n_j = n\) factors as a product of Poisson probabilities:

\[P(N_1 = n_1, \ldots, N_J = n_J) = \prod_j \frac{e^{-\lambda_j} \lambda_j^{n_j}}{n_j!} = e^{-\lambda} \frac{\prod_j \lambda_j^{n_j}}{\prod_j n_j!}.\]

Dividing by \(P(N = n) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^n / n!\) gives:

\[P(N_1 = n_1, \ldots, N_J = n_J \mid N = n) = \frac{n!}{\prod_j n_j!} \prod_j \left(\frac{\lambda_j}{\lambda}\right)^{n_j},\]

which is the \(\operatorname{Multinomial}(n;\, \lambda_1/\lambda, \ldots, \lambda_J/\lambda)\) probability mass function.

Application. At iteration \(k\), set \(\lambda_j = a_{ij}x_j^k\) as the predicted Poisson mean for the contribution of voxel \(j\) to bin \(i\). By the splitting property, conditioned on the observed count \(y_i\):

\[(n_{i1}, \ldots, n_{iJ}) \mid y_i,\, \mathbf{x}^k \;\sim\; \operatorname{Multinomial}\!\left(y_i;\; \frac{a_{i1}x_1^k}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}, \ldots, \frac{a_{iJ}x_J^k}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}\right),\]

where \(\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k) = \sum_j a_{ij}x_j^k + b_i\). Using the fact that \(E[N_j \mid N = n] = n \cdot p_j\) for a multinomial:

\[\boxed{\hat{n}_{ij}^k \;:=\; E[n_{ij} \mid y_i,\, \mathbf{x}^k] \;=\; \frac{a_{ij}\,x_j^k}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}\,y_i.}\]

NoteInterpretation

The formula apportions the observed count \(y_i\) among voxels in proportion to their predicted contributions \(a_{ij}x_j^k\). If voxel \(j\) is predicted to account for 30 % of the expected signal in bin \(i\), it is credited with 30 % of the observed photons — a probabilistic bookkeeping step that underpins every EM-type reconstruction algorithm.

TipBackground term \(b_i\)

If the background (scatter, randoms) is modelled as an additional independent Poisson source with known mean \(b_i\), it simply adds one more component to the multinomial split. Because \(b_i\) is a known constant rather than an unknown to be estimated, its component does not appear in \(Q\), and the formula above for the image voxels is unchanged.
