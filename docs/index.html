MLEM-FB Convergence

A self-contained proof for \(y \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b})\) and \(A\) having full column rank

1 Introduction

The MLEM (Maximum Likelihood Expectation Maximisation) algorithm is the standard reconstruction method in emission tomography (PET, SPECT). It produces non-negative images and steadily improves the fit to the measured data at every iteration. But does it always find the non-negative image with highest Poisson log-likelihood? And if so, why?

This document gives a complete, self-contained proof that MLEM converges to the unique non-negative image that maximises the Poisson log-likelihood. We assume only basic calculus and probability; every mathematical step is explained.

2 Setup

2.1 The forward model

We measure \(I\) detector counts \(y_1, \ldots, y_I \in \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}\) (the sinogram). The image to be recovered has \(J\) voxels with (unknown) activities \(x_1, \ldots, x_J \geq 0\), collected into a column vector \(\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^J\).

The forward model relates image to expected counts:

\[\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^{J} a_{ij}\, x_j + b_i = (A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b})_i, \qquad i = 1,\ldots,I,\]

where:

  • \(A \in \mathbb{R}^{I \times J}_{\geq 0}\) is the system matrix with entries \(a_{ij} \geq 0\). Entry \(a_{ij}\) is the probability that an event emitted in voxel \(j\) is detected in bin \(i\).
  • \(\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^I_{\geq 0}\) is the background (scatter and random coincidences), assumed known and fixed.

We model each detector bin as an independent Poisson random variable:

\[y_i \sim \operatorname{Poisson}\!\bigl(\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x})\bigr), \quad \text{independently for all } i.\]

2.2 The log-likelihood

The probability of observing count \(y_i\) when the expected count is \(\bar{y}_i\) is \(P(y_i) = e^{-\bar{y}_i}\,\bar{y}_i^{y_i}/y_i!\). Taking the logarithm and summing over all bins (and dropping terms that do not depend on \(\mathbf{x}\)) gives the Poisson log-likelihood:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_{i=1}^{I} \Bigl[y_i \log \bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x})\Bigr].\]

Our goal is to find the image \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) that maximises \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\) subject to \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\) (non-negativity).

2.3 The MLEM update

We define the sensitivity of voxel \(j\) as the total detection probability summed over all detector bins:

\[s_j = \sum_{i=1}^{I} a_{ij} > 0, \qquad j = 1,\ldots,J.\]

Starting from an initial image \(\mathbf{x}^0 > \mathbf{0}\) (all voxels strictly positive), MLEM produces the sequence \(\mathbf{x}^0, \mathbf{x}^1, \mathbf{x}^2, \ldots\) via the update rule:

\[\boxed{x_j^{k+1} = \frac{x_j^k}{s_j} \sum_{i=1}^{I} a_{ij}\, \frac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}, \qquad j = 1,\ldots,J.}\]

The ratio \(y_i / \bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)\) compares measured to predicted counts in bin \(i\): it equals 1 when the current estimate is perfect, is greater than 1 when the model under-predicts, and less than 1 when it over-predicts. These correction factors are back-projected into image space and normalised by the voxel sensitivity \(s_j\).

2.4 The full-rank assumption

We assume throughout that \(A\) has full column rank, meaning no non-zero vector \(\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}\) satisfies \(A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}\). Practically this means:

  1. Every voxel contributes to at least one detector bin (\(s_j > 0\) for all \(j\)).
  2. No two voxels have identical detection patterns.

2.5 Two types of constrained maximum

A crucial distinction runs throughout the proof. The constrained maximum \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) can be of two types, distinguished by whether the unconstrained maximum of \(\mathcal{L}\) is feasible.

NoteCase (i): Interior solution — all voxels active (\(\hat{\mathbf{x}} > \mathbf{0}\))

The constrained and unconstrained maxima coincide. The gradient of \(\mathcal{L}\) vanishes at \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\):

\[\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_j}\bigg|_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}} = \sum_i a_{ij}\,\frac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i(\hat{\mathbf{x}})} - s_j = 0 \quad \text{for all } j.\]

This means the average correction ratio \(\sum_i a_{ij} y_i/\bar{y}_i / \sum_i a_{ij}\) equals exactly 1 for every voxel.

NoteCase (ii): Boundary solution — some voxels are zero (some \(\hat{x}_j = 0\))

The constrained maximum lies on the boundary of the non-negative orthant: the unconstrained maximum has some negative components and is infeasible. The Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) optimality conditions hold:

\[\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_j}\bigg|_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}} = \sum_i a_{ij}\,\frac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i(\hat{\mathbf{x}})} - s_j \begin{cases} = 0 & \text{if } \hat{x}_j > 0 \quad \text{(active voxel)},\\[4pt] \leq 0 & \text{if } \hat{x}_j = 0 \quad \text{(inactive voxel)}. \end{cases}\]

For an inactive voxel, the gradient is non-positive: increasing \(x_j\) from zero would decrease \(\mathcal{L}\), so zeroing that voxel is optimal.

Under the full-rank assumption \(\mathcal{L}\) is strictly concave (shown in Step 5), so in both cases \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) is unique.

3 Proof Roadmap

NoteThe five things we need to show

  1. Bounded above: \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\) has a finite upper bound over \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\).

  2. Non-negativity preserved: \(\mathbf{x}^k \geq \mathbf{0}\) implies \(\mathbf{x}^{k+1} \geq \mathbf{0}\); and \(\mathbf{x}^0 > \mathbf{0}\) implies \(\mathbf{x}^k > \mathbf{0}\) for all \(k\).

  3. Monotonic increase: \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\), with strict inequality whenever \(\mathbf{x}^k \neq \hat{\mathbf{x}}\).

  4. Bounded iterates: \(\{\mathbf{x}^k\}\) remains in a compact set.

  5. Convergence: \(\mathbf{x}^k \to \hat{\mathbf{x}}\) as \(k \to \infty\).

Steps 1–3 imply that \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\) is monotonically increasing and bounded above, so it converges to some value \(\mathcal{L}^*\). Steps 4 and 5 show that the iterates themselves converge to \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\).

4 Step 1: The Log-Likelihood is Bounded Above

The Poisson log-likelihood can be rewritten as:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i \bigl(y_i \log y_i - y_i\bigr) - \underbrace{\sum_i \Bigl[y_i \log\tfrac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i} + \bar{y}_i - y_i\Bigr]}_{\geq\; 0}.\]

To see this, write \(y_i \log \bar{y}_i = y_i \log y_i - y_i\log(y_i/\bar{y}_i)\) and rearrange. The underlined term is a sum of generalised KL divergences \(d(y_i, \bar{y}_i) = y_i \log(y_i/\bar{y}_i) + \bar{y}_i - y_i \geq 0\) (each follows from \(\log u \leq u - 1\) with \(u = \bar{y}_i/y_i\)). Therefore:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \leq \sum_i \bigl(y_i \log y_i - y_i\bigr) =: \mathcal{L}_{\max} < \infty. \qquad \square\]

TipConvention: \(0 \log 0 = 0\)

When \(y_i = 0\) the term \(y_i \log y_i\) is formally \(0 \cdot (-\infty)\). We adopt \(0 \log 0 := 0\), justified by \(\lim_{y\to 0^+} y\log y = 0\).

5 Step 2: MLEM Preserves Non-Negativity

Inspect the update: if \(x_j^k \geq 0\), then \(x_j^{k+1} = x_j^k \cdot (\text{non-negative factor}) \geq 0\). By induction, \(\mathbf{x}^k \geq \mathbf{0}\) for all \(k\).

If \(\mathbf{x}^0 > \mathbf{0}\), then \(x_j^{k+1} = x_j^k \cdot (\text{strictly positive factor}) > 0\). Every iterate is strictly positive. \(\square\)

6 Step 3: Each MLEM Update Increases the Log-Likelihood

This is the most important step, using the EM framework.

6.1 The complete-data idea

Maximising \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\) directly is hard because \(\log \bar{y}_i = \log(\sum_j a_{ij} x_j + b_i)\) couples all unknowns. The EM trick is to imagine we could also observe which voxel each detected event originated from — information the detector does not record.

Define the complete data \(n_{ij}\) as the (unobserved) number of events detected in bin \(i\) from voxel \(j\), and \(n_{i0}\) for background:

\[n_{ij} \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(a_{ij}\, x_j), \quad j \geq 1, \qquad n_{i0} \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(b_i), \qquad \text{all independent,}\]

with \(y_i = \sum_{j=0}^{J} n_{ij}\). The complete-data log-likelihood decouples over voxels:

\[\log p(\mathbf{n} \mid \mathbf{x}) = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \left[\log x_j \underbrace{\sum_i n_{ij}}_{\text{events from }j} - x_j \underbrace{\sum_i a_{ij}}_{s_j}\right] + \text{const.}\]

6.2 The \(Q\) function

Since \(\mathbf{n}\) is not observed, EM replaces \(\log p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{x})\) by its conditional expectation given the data \(\mathbf{y}\) and current estimate \(\mathbf{x}^k\):

\[Q(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{x}^k) = E\!\left[\log p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{x}) \;\Big|\; \mathbf{y},\, \mathbf{x}^k\right].\]

Using the Poisson splitting property (if independent Poisson variables sum to \(n\), their conditional joint distribution is Multinomial with probabilities proportional to their means - see Section 10.1), one shows:

\[E[n_{ij} \mid y_i, \mathbf{x}^k] = \frac{a_{ij}\, x_j^k}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}\, y_i =: \hat{n}_{ij}^k.\]

The observed count \(y_i\) is apportioned among voxels in proportion to their predicted contributions. Substituting:

\[Q(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{x}^k) = \sum_{j=1}^{J} \left[\hat{n}_j^k \log x_j - s_j\, x_j\right] + \text{const}, \qquad \hat{n}_j^k = \sum_i \hat{n}_{ij}^k.\]

This is separable in \(x_j\): each voxel can be updated independently.

6.3 The fundamental identity

From \(p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{x}) = p(\mathbf{y}\mid\mathbf{x})\cdot p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x})\), taking logarithms and then the conditional expectation \(E[\,\cdot\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}^k]\):

\[Q(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) = \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) + H(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k),\]

where \(H(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) = E[\log p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x})\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}^k]\). Rearranging gives the fundamental identity:

\[\boxed{\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) = Q(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) - H(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k).}\]

6.4 The EM guarantee: \(\mathcal{L}\) never decreases

The M-step sets \(\mathbf{x}^{k+1} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{x}\geq\mathbf{0}} Q(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\). Using the fundamental identity at \(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}\) and \(\mathbf{x}^k\):

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}) - \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) = \underbrace{\bigl[Q(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) - Q(\mathbf{x}^k\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\bigr]}_{\text{Term A}\;\geq\;0} + \underbrace{\bigl[H(\mathbf{x}^k\mid\mathbf{x}^k) - H(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\bigr]}_{\text{Term B}\;\geq\;0}.\]

Term A \(\geq 0\): \(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}\) maximises \(Q(\cdot\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\) by construction.

Term B \(\geq 0\): The difference equals \(D_{\mathrm{KL}}\!\bigl(p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}^k)\,\|\, p(\mathbf{n}\mid\mathbf{y},\mathbf{x}^{k+1})\bigr) \geq 0\) (KL divergence is always non-negative).

Therefore \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\). \(\square\)

NoteWhy the KL divergence is non-negative

\(D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) = \sum_{\mathbf{n}} p(\mathbf{n})\log\frac{p(\mathbf{n})}{q(\mathbf{n})} \geq 0\) follows from Jensen’s inequality applied to the concave function \(\log\):

\[D_{\mathrm{KL}}(p\|q) = -\sum_{\mathbf{n}} p(\mathbf{n})\log\frac{q(\mathbf{n})}{p(\mathbf{n})} \geq -\log\!\left(\sum_{\mathbf{n}} p(\mathbf{n})\cdot\frac{q(\mathbf{n})}{p(\mathbf{n})}\right) = -\log 1 = 0.\]

6.5 The M-step gives the MLEM update

Maximise \(Q(\mathbf{x}\mid\mathbf{x}^k) = \sum_j [\hat{n}_j^k \log x_j - s_j x_j]\) over \(x_j \geq 0\). Each term is strictly concave in \(x_j\) with an unconstrained maximum at \(x_j = \hat{n}_j^k / s_j > 0\). Since this is already non-negative, the constrained maximum equals the unconstrained one:

\[\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x_j} = \frac{\hat{n}_j^k}{x_j} - s_j = 0 \;\Longrightarrow\; x_j^{k+1} = \frac{\hat{n}_j^k}{s_j} = \frac{x_j^k}{s_j} \sum_i a_{ij}\,\frac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)},\]

which is exactly the MLEM formula. \(\square\)

TipWhen does \(\mathcal{L}\) increase strictly? The role of Case (i) vs Case (ii)

The EM guarantee gives \(\geq\), but for convergence we need to know when the increase is strict.

Term A = 0 iff \(\mathbf{x}^{k+1} = \mathbf{x}^k\), i.e. \(\mathbf{x}^k\) already maximises \(Q(\cdot\mid\mathbf{x}^k)\). Since the unconstrained maximiser of \(Q\) is \(x_j = \hat{n}_j^k/s_j > 0\) (always interior and unique), this requires \(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial x_j\big|_{\mathbf{x}^k} = 0\) for all \(j\), i.e. \(\mathbf{x}^k\) is the unconstrained maximum of \(\mathcal{L}\).

  • Case (i) — interior solution: the unconstrained and constrained maxima coincide at \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\). Term A = 0 iff \(\mathbf{x}^k = \hat{\mathbf{x}}\). The log-likelihood increases strictly at every iteration until \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) is reached.

  • Case (ii) — boundary solution: the unconstrained maximum of \(\mathcal{L}\) has infeasible (negative) components and lies outside \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\). Therefore no feasible \(\mathbf{x}^k > \mathbf{0}\) satisfies \(\nabla\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) = \mathbf{0}\), and Term A \(> 0\) for every iterate. The log-likelihood increases strictly at every single step — the algorithm never stops making progress in finite time.

7 Step 4: The Iterates Are Bounded

From Steps 1 and 3, \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\) is monotonically increasing. All iterates therefore lie in the super-level set:

\[\mathcal{S} = \{\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0} : \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^0)\}.\]

We now show \(\mathcal{S}\) is bounded (and hence compact, since it is also closed).

Why \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \to -\infty\) as \(\|\mathbf{x}\| \to \infty\): \(\mathcal{L}\) contains the term \(-\sum_i \bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}) = -\mathbf{1}^\top A\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{1}^\top\mathbf{b}\). When \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\) and \(\|\mathbf{x}\| \to \infty\), at least one \(x_j \to \infty\). Since \(A\) has full column rank, \(\sum_i a_{ij} = s_j > 0\), so \(s_j x_j \to \infty\), driving \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \to -\infty\).

Since \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \to -\infty\) outside any ball but \(\mathcal{S}\) requires \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^0) > -\infty\), the set \(\mathcal{S}\) must be bounded. All iterates \(\mathbf{x}^k\) lie in \(\mathcal{S}\). \(\square\)

8 Step 5: Convergence to the Constrained ML Solution

We now show \(\mathbf{x}^k \to \hat{\mathbf{x}} = \arg\max_{\mathbf{x}\geq\mathbf{0}}\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\).

8.1 Strict concavity and uniqueness of \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\)

The Hessian of \(\mathcal{L}\) is:

\[\nabla^2\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}) = -A^\top D(\mathbf{x})\,A, \qquad D(\mathbf{x}) = \operatorname{diag}\!\left(\frac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x})^2}\right) > 0.\]

Because \(A\) has full column rank, \(A^\top D A\) is positive definite for any diagonal \(D > 0\), so \(\nabla^2\mathcal{L}\) is negative definite: \(\mathcal{L}\) is strictly concave. A strictly concave function over a convex set has at most one maximum, so \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) is unique.

8.2 \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) is a fixed point of MLEM in both cases

We show \(F(\hat{\mathbf{x}}) = \hat{\mathbf{x}}\), where \(F\) denotes one MLEM step.

Active voxels (\(\hat{x}_j > 0\), both cases): The KKT condition gives \(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial x_j\big|_{\hat{\mathbf{x}}} = 0\), which means \(\sum_i a_{ij} y_i/\bar{y}_i(\hat{\mathbf{x}}) = s_j\). The MLEM correction ratio equals 1, so \(F(\hat{\mathbf{x}})_j = \hat{x}_j \cdot 1 = \hat{x}_j\). \(\checkmark\)

Inactive voxels (\(\hat{x}_j = 0\), Case (ii) only): The multiplicative formula gives \(F(\hat{\mathbf{x}})_j = 0 \times (\ldots) = 0 = \hat{x}_j\), regardless of the gradient. MLEM cannot create activity from nothing. \(\checkmark\)

The gradient does not need to be zero at an inactive voxel — the algorithm keeps it at zero through the multiplicative structure alone.

8.3 The log-likelihood values converge

From Steps 1 and 3: \(\{\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\}\) is monotonically increasing and bounded above. By the monotone convergence theorem it converges:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) \;\longrightarrow\; \mathcal{L}^* \;\leq\; \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}}).\]

We will show \(\mathcal{L}^* = \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\).

8.4 A convergent subsequence exists

Since all iterates lie in the compact set \(\mathcal{S}\), the Bolzano–Weierstrass theorem (every bounded sequence in \(\mathbb{R}^J\) has a convergent subsequence) guarantees a subsequence:

\[\mathbf{x}^{k_m} \;\longrightarrow\; \mathbf{x}^* \in \mathcal{S}.\]

Because \(\mathbf{x}^{k_m}\) is a subsequence of \(\{\mathbf{x}^k\}\) and \(\mathcal{L}\) is continuous:

\[\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \lim_{m\to\infty}\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k_m}) = \mathcal{L}^*.\]

(Two facts combine: a subsequence of a convergent sequence shares its limit; \(\mathcal{L}\) is continuous so the limit commutes with the function.)

8.5 The limit point is the constrained maximum: \(\mathcal{L}^* = \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\)

Suppose for contradiction that \(\mathcal{L}^* < \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\), so \(\mathbf{x}^* \neq \hat{\mathbf{x}}\). We show this leads to a contradiction in both cases.

NoteCase (i): Interior solution (\(\hat{\mathbf{x}} > \mathbf{0}\))

From Step 2, all iterates satisfy \(\mathbf{x}^k > \mathbf{0}\), so \(\mathbf{x}^* \geq \mathbf{0}\). Since \(\hat{\mathbf{x}} > \mathbf{0}\), a boundary accumulation point is in principle possible but let us consider both sub-cases.

If \(\mathbf{x}^* > \mathbf{0}\) (interior): A fixed point of \(F\) in the interior requires \(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial x_j = 0\) for all \(j\). By strict concavity the only such point is \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\). Since \(\mathbf{x}^* \neq \hat{\mathbf{x}}\), we have \(F(\mathbf{x}^*) \neq \mathbf{x}^*\), so Term A \(> 0\) and:

\[\mathcal{L}(F(\mathbf{x}^*)) > \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathcal{L}^*.\]

Since \(F\) and \(\mathcal{L}\) are continuous: \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k_m+1}) = \mathcal{L}(F(\mathbf{x}^{k_m})) \to \mathcal{L}(F(\mathbf{x}^*)) > \mathcal{L}^*\). But \(\mathbf{x}^{k_m+1}\) is a subsequence of \(\{\mathbf{x}^k\}\), whose \(\mathcal{L}\)-values converge to \(\mathcal{L}^*\). Contradiction.

If \(\mathbf{x}^*\) has some zero components: Since \(\hat{\mathbf{x}} > \mathbf{0}\) and \(\mathbf{x}^* \neq \hat{\mathbf{x}}\), there is some voxel \(j\) where \(x_j^* \neq \hat{x}_j\). For the active voxels of \(\mathbf{x}^*\), the same fixed-point argument applies; for the zero voxels of \(\mathbf{x}^*\), the gradient \(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial x_j\big|_{\mathbf{x}^*} > 0\) (since \(\hat{x}_j > 0\) and \(\mathcal{L}\) increases toward \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\)), so \(F(\mathbf{x}^*)_j = 0\) yet \(F(\mathbf{x}^k)_j > 0\) for \(\mathbf{x}^k\) nearby, meaning \(F(\mathbf{x}^*) \neq \mathbf{x}^*\). The same contradiction follows.

In all sub-cases, \(\mathcal{L}^* < \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\) leads to a contradiction. Therefore \(\mathcal{L}^* = \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\).

NoteCase (ii): Boundary solution (some \(\hat{x}_j = 0\))

Step A — any interior accumulation point leads to a contradiction. From Step 2, all iterates \(\mathbf{x}^k > \mathbf{0}\). Suppose \(\mathbf{x}^* > \mathbf{0}\) (interior). As shown in Step 3, no interior point is a fixed point of \(F\) in Case (ii) (the unconstrained maximum of \(\mathcal{L}\) is infeasible). Therefore \(F(\mathbf{x}^*) \neq \mathbf{x}^*\) and Term A \(> 0\), giving \(\mathcal{L}(F(\mathbf{x}^*)) > \mathcal{L}^*\). By continuity, \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k_m+1}) \to \mathcal{L}(F(\mathbf{x}^*)) > \mathcal{L}^*\), contradicting \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k) \to \mathcal{L}^*\). So \(\mathbf{x}^*\) must be on the boundary (some \(x_j^* = 0\)).

Step B — the boundary accumulation point satisfies the KKT conditions. Consider a voxel \(j\) with \(x_j^* = 0\). Because \(\mathbf{x}^{k_m} > \mathbf{0}\) and \(x_j^{k_m} \to 0\), the MLEM update gives:

\[x_j^{k_m+1} = x_j^{k_m} \cdot \frac{1}{s_j}\sum_i a_{ij}\frac{y_i}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^{k_m})}.\]

If \(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial x_j\big|_{\mathbf{x}^*} > 0\), the correction ratio exceeds 1 near \(\mathbf{x}^*\), so \(x_j^{k_m+1} > x_j^{k_m}\). The sequence is being pushed away from zero at every step near \(\mathbf{x}^*\), which is incompatible with \(x_j^{k_m} \to 0\). Therefore:

\[\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial x_j}\bigg|_{\mathbf{x}^*} \leq 0 \quad \text{for all zero components.}\]

For positive components \(x_j^* > 0\), the same fixed-point argument as in Case (i) shows \(\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial x_j\big|_{\mathbf{x}^*} = 0\).

Together, \(\mathbf{x}^*\) satisfies the KKT conditions for \(\max_{\mathbf{x}\geq\mathbf{0}}\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\). Since \(\mathcal{L}\) is strictly concave, the KKT conditions are both necessary and sufficient for a global maximum, and the maximum is unique. Therefore \(\mathbf{x}^* = \hat{\mathbf{x}}\) — contradicting \(\mathcal{L}^* = \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*) < \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\).

In both steps, \(\mathcal{L}^* < \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\) leads to a contradiction. Therefore \(\mathcal{L}^* = \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\).

8.6 \(\mathbf{x}^*\) equals \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\)

Having shown \(\mathcal{L}^* = \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\), any accumulation point \(\mathbf{x}^*\) satisfies \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\). Since \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) is the unique constrained maximum (strict concavity), \(\mathbf{x}^* = \hat{\mathbf{x}}\).

8.7 The full sequence converges

Every convergent subsequence of \(\{\mathbf{x}^k\}\) shares the same limit \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) by the argument above: any accumulation point achieves \(\mathcal{L}^* = \mathcal{L}(\hat{\mathbf{x}})\), and strict concavity forces it to equal \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\). Since the sequence is bounded (Step 4), this single accumulation point forces the full sequence to converge:

\[\mathbf{x}^k \;\longrightarrow\; \hat{\mathbf{x}} \quad \text{as } k \to \infty. \qquad \square\]

9 Summary

NoteConvergence theorem

Let \(y_i \sim \operatorname{Poisson}((A\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{b})_i)\) with \(A\) having full column rank and \(b_i \geq 0\). Starting from any \(\mathbf{x}^0 > \mathbf{0}\), the MLEM iterates \(\mathbf{x}^k\) converge to the unique constrained maximum \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) of the Poisson log-likelihood over \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\).

Step What we showed Key tool
1 \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x})\) bounded above by \(\mathcal{L}_{\max}\) \(\log u \leq u-1\) (generalised KL)
2 \(\mathbf{x}^k > \mathbf{0}\) for all \(k\) (from \(\mathbf{x}^0 > \mathbf{0}\)) Multiplicative structure of MLEM
3 \(\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^{k+1}) > \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{x}^k)\) for \(\mathbf{x}^k \neq \hat{\mathbf{x}}\) EM framework (\(Q\) function + KL \(\geq 0\))
4 \(\{\mathbf{x}^k\} \subset \mathcal{S}\) (compact) Full rank \(\Rightarrow\) coercivity of \(\mathcal{L}\)
5 \(\mathbf{x}^k \to \hat{\mathbf{x}}\) Bolzano–Weierstrass + strict concavity + KKT

Step 3 is the heart of the proof: the EM framework guarantees progress at every step, with the KL divergence as the reason. Steps 4 and 5 ensure this progress is not wasted — the sequence stays bounded and the accumulated improvements converge to the correct answer, whether \(\hat{\mathbf{x}}\) lies in the interior of \(\mathbf{x} \geq \mathbf{0}\) (all voxels active) or on its boundary (some voxels zeroed out).

10 Appendix

10.1 The conditional expectation via Poisson splitting

Setup. In the complete-data model each voxel \(j\) contributes independently to detector bin \(i\): \(n_{ij} \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(a_{ij}x_j)\), and the observed count is the total \(y_i = \sum_j n_{ij}\).

Poisson splitting property. Let \(N_1, \ldots, N_J\) be independent Poisson random variables with means \(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_J\), and let \(N = N_1 + \cdots + N_J \sim \operatorname{Poisson}(\lambda)\), \(\lambda = \sum_j \lambda_j\). Then the conditional distribution of \((N_1, \ldots, N_J)\) given \(N = n\) is multinomial:

\[(N_1, \ldots, N_J) \mid N = n \;\sim\; \operatorname{Multinomial}\!\left(n;\; \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda}, \ldots, \frac{\lambda_J}{\lambda}\right).\]

Proof. The joint probability of \((N_1 = n_1, \ldots, N_J = n_J)\) with \(\sum_j n_j = n\) factors as a product of Poisson probabilities:

\[P(N_1 = n_1, \ldots, N_J = n_J) = \prod_j \frac{e^{-\lambda_j} \lambda_j^{n_j}}{n_j!} = e^{-\lambda} \frac{\prod_j \lambda_j^{n_j}}{\prod_j n_j!}.\]

Dividing by \(P(N = n) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^n / n!\) gives:

\[P(N_1 = n_1, \ldots, N_J = n_J \mid N = n) = \frac{n!}{\prod_j n_j!} \prod_j \left(\frac{\lambda_j}{\lambda}\right)^{n_j},\]

which is the \(\operatorname{Multinomial}(n;\, \lambda_1/\lambda, \ldots, \lambda_J/\lambda)\) probability mass function.

Application. At iteration \(k\), set \(\lambda_j = a_{ij}x_j^k\) as the predicted Poisson mean for the contribution of voxel \(j\) to bin \(i\). By the splitting property, conditioned on the observed count \(y_i\):

\[(n_{i1}, \ldots, n_{iJ}) \mid y_i,\, \mathbf{x}^k \;\sim\; \operatorname{Multinomial}\!\left(y_i;\; \frac{a_{i1}x_1^k}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}, \ldots, \frac{a_{iJ}x_J^k}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}\right),\]

where \(\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k) = \sum_j a_{ij}x_j^k + b_i\). Using the fact that \(E[N_j \mid N = n] = n \cdot p_j\) for a multinomial:

\[\boxed{\hat{n}_{ij}^k \;:=\; E[n_{ij} \mid y_i,\, \mathbf{x}^k] \;=\; \frac{a_{ij}\,x_j^k}{\bar{y}_i(\mathbf{x}^k)}\,y_i.}\]

NoteInterpretation

The formula apportions the observed count \(y_i\) among voxels in proportion to their predicted contributions \(a_{ij}x_j^k\). If voxel \(j\) is predicted to account for 30 % of the expected signal in bin \(i\), it is credited with 30 % of the observed photons — a probabilistic bookkeeping step that underpins every EM-type reconstruction algorithm.

TipBackground term \(b_i\)

If the background (scatter, randoms) is modelled as an additional independent Poisson source with known mean \(b_i\), it simply adds one more component to the multinomial split. Because \(b_i\) is a known constant rather than an unknown to be estimated, its component does not appear in \(Q\), and the formula above for the image voxels is unchanged.
